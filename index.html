Tribute to Amelia Earhart

Amelia Earhart

The stars seemed near enough to touch and never before have I seen so many. I always believed the lure of flying is the lure of beauty, but I was sure of it that night. -Amelia Earhart

An American aviation pioneer, Amelia Earhart (born July 24th, 1897). Earhart at an early age developed a passion for adventure. Earhart took her first flying lesson on January of 1921. Six months later, she managed to save enough money to buy her first plane. A used Kinner Airster biplane, she named Canary. Earhart and with the help of Canary, she was able to achieve her first women's record by climbing to an altitude of 14,000 feet. But that was just the start.

Below is a list of some of her accomplishments:

  • October 22, 1922 – Earhart broke the women’s altitude record when she climbed to an altitude of 14,000 feet
  • June 17-18, 1928 – She was the first woman to fly across the Atlantic in a time of 20hrs 40min
  • June 25, 1930 – She set the women’s speed record for 100 kilometers with no load and with a load of 500 kilograms
  • July 5, 1930 – Set speed record for of 181mph over a 3K course
  • May 20-21, 1932 – Earhart was the first woman to fly solo across the Atlantic in a time of 14hrs 56min
  • August 24-25, 1932 – First woman to fly solo nonstop coast to coast. Also set the women’s nonstop transcontinental speed record, flying 2,448 miles in 19hrs 5min
  • July 7-8, 1933 – She broke her previous transcontinental speed record by making the same flight in 17hrs 7min
  • April l9–20, 1935 – First person to fly solo from Los Angeles to Mexico City in 13hrs 23min
  • May 8, 1935 – First person to fly solo nonstop from Mexico City to Newark in 14hrs 19min

On June 1st, Earhart started out on her biggest adventure. Accompanied by her navigator, Fred Noonan, the two departed from Miami and began a 29,000-mile journey around the world. Unfortunately, on July 2nd, 1937, Earhart and Noonan took off from Lea Airfield with just 7,000 miles left of their journey. Their intended destination was Howland Island for their next fueling. As dawn neared, Earhart called the ITASCA, reporting cloudy weather. In later transmissions, Earhart asked the ITASCA to take bearings on her. The ITASCA sent her a steady stream of transmissions, but Earhart was unable to hear them. Her radio transmissions, irregular through most of the flight, were faint or interrupted with static. Around 7:40 am, the Itasca picked up the message stating: “Fuel is running low. Been unable to reach you by radio. We are flying at 1,000 feet.” The ship tried to reply, but were unable to make contact. At 8:45 am, Earhart reported: “We are running north and south”. Nothing further was heard from her.

Despite many theories, no proof of Earhart’s fate exists. However, there is no doubt that the world will always remember Amelia Earhart for her courage, vision and groundbreaking achievements, for both aviation and women.
